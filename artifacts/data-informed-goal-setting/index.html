EDEL 518A Data Project · Standard 4.6

Qualitative and Quantitative Data for Student Goal Setting

A two-page resource that helps 12th-grade Government students interpret multiple forms of learning evidence, make a responsible decision, and take ownership of a measurable goal.

What Students Do

Page 1 · Interpret evidence

Qualitative vs. Quantitative Data

Students compare words and observations—such as reflections, interviews and classroom notes—with numbers and counts—such as assessment scores, attendance and completed assignments. They then use Notice–Ask–Act to identify patterns, consider what might explain them, and select a next step.

  • Look for patterns rather than labels
  • Consider language, access, health and responsibilities
  • Ask what the numbers do not show
  • Use data to support growth rather than judge worth

Page 2 · Act on evidence

Goal Setting With Data

Students select one quantitative data point and one qualitative observation, write a SMART goal, choose a strategy and support, decide how success will be measured, and reflect during a later check-in.

  • Specific, measurable and time-bound goal
  • Strategy and support plan
  • Clear measure of success
  • Reflection and adjustment

Direct standard alignment

Connections to 4.6.a–c

4.6.a

Responsible Data Practices

The resource models fair interpretation, attention to context and private handling of student responses.

4.6.b

Multiple Forms of Evidence

Students combine a number with an observation and use both to make a learning decision.

4.6.c

Student Ownership

Students use their evidence to set, monitor and revise a goal instead of receiving a goal chosen for them.

Sharing plan

Using the Resource With Colleagues

I will share the project as a ready-to-adapt routine for student data conversations. Colleagues can revise the examples for their subjects while keeping the same sequence: combine quantitative and qualitative evidence, interpret it in context, choose a support, and give students ownership of the next step. I will also invite discussion about what data are appropriate to collect, how responses will be protected, and what feedback would improve the resource.
